Escalation Has a Reputation Problem

Lee Ball · 27 Apr 2026 · 2 min read

An escalation, handled well, should never be anything to fear.

Escalation has a reputation problem. In too many organisations, it's treated as a complaint - a signal that something has gone wrong, that the team has failed, that a customer is unhappy enough to go over someone's head. That perception shapes everything that follows. It drives defensive behaviour, encourages people to hide problems, and creates a culture where the instinct is to contain rather than to solve.

The fear of escalation is corrosive. When people believe that raising a problem reflects badly on them, they stop raising problems - trying one more thing, waiting a little longer, looking for a process to hide behind rather than a solution to reach for. The irony is that this behaviour, designed to avoid the appearance of failure, almost always makes things worse. Problems that could have been resolved quickly become entrenched, customers who might have been satisfied with a timely and honest conversation become frustrated by delay and deflection, and the team, rather than developing better judgment, learns only to be more defensive.

Done properly, escalation is none of those things. It is a structured, confident, upward delegation - a recognition that a decision needs to be made at a level above the team, whether that's about prioritisation, resource, or the shape of a solution. It is the system working as it should. A team that escalates well has typically exhausted the options within their control, formed a clear view of what they need, and is asking for help in a deliberate and considered way. That should be recognised as good judgment, not a failure. Equally, a customer escalating well will be doing it in coordination with the team and illustrating great partnership.

Changing that perception is a leadership problem before it is anything else. If the culture around escalation is defensive, it's because somewhere along the line people learned that raising problems had consequences. The signals that create that culture are often subtle - a manager who reacts badly when an issue surfaces, a metric that rewards containment over resolution, a process so rigid that stepping outside it feels like a risk. Reversing it requires those same signals to change. Escalation needs to be explicitly welcomed, modelled from the top, and separated from any notion of blame. When a senior leader escalates visibly and without embarrassment, it gives everyone else permission to do the same.

The teams that get this right don't just resolve issues faster. They build trust - with customers, with each other, and with the people above them in the organisation. That trust is what allows difficult conversations to happen early, when they're still manageable, rather than late, when they're not. An escalation, handled well, should never be anything to fear.
